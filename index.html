Up, Up, Ever Up!

Written By: Anita Yasuda

Who is Anita Yasuda

Anita Yasuda was born in 1968 and is the author of the book Up, Up, Ever Up! Junko Tabei: A Life In The Mountains, illustrated by Yuko Shimizu. She has also written many other books for young readers, including Diwali: A Festival Of Joy, illustrated by Darshika Varma, Bollywood Beat, illustrated by Devika Oza, and My Thali Is A Song.

Anita earned her honours degree in East Asian and South Asian studies at the University of Toronto, Victoria College, and later received her Montessori and Early Childhood qualifications at the AMI Institute in Vancouver, British Columbia.

After graduating, she worked many different jobs, from preschool teacher to sales clerk, caterer, firefighter for one day, freelance magazine writer, and author of books for educational publishers.

Today, Anita is a member of CANSCAIP, the Writers Union of Canada, and SCBWI, and she serves as a co-host of SCBWI Canada-East BIPOC Talk.

Synopsis

The book Up Up Ever Up starts with the main character, Junko Tabei, under a hill surrounded by vibrant sakura trees and cold grass, listening to stories about colossal mountains. Junko developed the urge to climb. As soon as she turned 10, Junko and her friends climbed Mount Chausu. Afterwards, when Junko left her home in Miharu and settled in Tokyo, where she felt like a fish out of water, she longed for the mountains. All the mountain climbing clubs she found said "No Women Allowed," but she eventually found one that said yes. In the weeks that followed, Junko would climb every chance she got and, in doing so, eventually found someone just like her, so they got married. Junko and her partner set their sights on the greatest challenge of their lives, climbing Everest, a summit no woman had ever accomplished. Junko began recycling old scraps to construct climbing gear specifically for women, as equipment at the time was only for men. In the years that ensued, Junko finally completed her preparation for the Everest climb with her team, and so they landed in Nepal and began their hike with many mothers and young girls watching. As Everest loomed above them, unforgiving and vast, an avalanche came down, causing devastating damage to their equipment and the only remaining oxygen for Junko and her guide, Ang Tsering Sherpa. Nearly two weeks after the disaster, they finally arrived at the last camp before the summit. Junko began the ascent for the final stretch, "Up Up and Ever Up," with the last of her strength. Standing at the pinnacle of the world, she had accomplished what no woman had ever, conquered Everest.

About Junko Tabei

Junko was born on September 22, 1939, in Miharu, Fukushima, Japan. She was a Japanese mountaineer, author, and teacher. In Miharu, she was the fifth daughter of seven children and was considered a frail child. At the age of 10, is when she first started climbing on a class trip to Mount Nasu. Unfortunately, Junko's family was too poor to fund her passion for climbing.

Tabei studied English and American literature at Showa Women's University and married fellow mountaineer Masanobu Tabei at age 27. They had two children, a daughter and a son. In 1969, Junko founded the Joshi-Tohan Club, a women-only mountaineering club.

To fund her climbing, she taught piano lessons and sewed her own gear from scratch. On May 16, 1975, alongside her Sherpa guide Ang Tsering, she became the first woman to summit Everest after surviving an avalanche during the expedition, and later became the first woman to complete the Seven Summits. Later in her life, she organized clean-up expeditions on Everest and led Mount Fuji climbs for youth affected by the 2011 earthquake.

Facts about Mount Everest

Everest's Massive Peak

Mount Everest is the world's highest peak at 8,848.86 meters (29,031.7 feet) above sea level.

Names in Different Cultures

Known as Sagarmatha in Nepal ("head of the great blue sky") and Chomolungma in Tibet ("Mother Goddess of the World"), it was named "Everest" in 1865 after Sir George Everest.

It is still Growing

Due to the collision of the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates, Everest grows higher by about a quarter of an inch (0.1 inches) every year.

Overall Theme

"Technique and ability alone do not get you to the top; it is the willpower that is the most important. This willpower you cannot buy with money or be given by others... it rises from your heart." - Junko Tabei

What's the Theme

One of the major themes I notice in Up, Up, Ever Up is the perseverance of somebody who never gives up. Junko is a prime example of this, as she has been rejected from climbing clubs, financial droughts, sexism, and even a life-threatening avalanche, yet she has never seen these setbacks as a loss but a reason to keep persevering. The book explores concepts such as gender barriers and shows how women in Junko's time were constantly told that climbing and ambition were not for them. Belonging is another theme I found important, since Junko never feels at home in the city but finds peace and purpose on the mountain. These themes together show a woman who refused to let the world decide what she could and could not do.

Symbolism

In my opinion, the mountains in this book mean far more than just a physical challenge. They represent every barrier and obstacle Junko had to push through, and every summit she reaches feels like another wall she overcame. The sakura trees at the beginning of the story represent her home, and the childhood wonder that started it all, which makes her reaching the top of Everest feel even more powerful.

Historical Context

What stood out to me was how out of the ordinary Junko's Everest summit in 1975 really was. Women at the time were shut out of professional mountaineering, told to stay at home, and dismissed by sponsors and clubs. Junko's achievement was not just a win for her, but a message to the entire world that women belonged everywhere, even at the top of the highest mountain on Earth.

The Tale of Sisyphus

Up, Up, Ever Up connects to the tale of Sisyphus in an interesting way. Like Sisyphus, Junko faced endless obstacles that pushed her back down, from being rejected by climbing clubs, to surviving an avalanche, to being told women should be raising children instead of climbing. But unlike Sisyphus, whose struggle is eternal and meaningless, Junko's perseverance had purpose and eventually led her to the summit. Where Sisyphus is a symbol of futile struggle, Junko transforms that same relentless pushing forward into triumph.

Thoughts

Reaction (Informal-ish)

When I first started reading the book, I immediately noticed the eye-catching illustrations and became heavily intrigued by what came next. Honestly, Up, Up, Ever Up, was my chosen TTT from when I first saw it. The reason is that I am facinated in asian culture and themes associated with it (asian culture), such as anime, manga, food, games, and much more.

Everything about this book was amazing to the very minute detail, with the only downside being its length. But other than that, the most intriguing details were the illustrations (mountains and sakura trees, for example), the depth in the story, and the impact of the deeply rooted sexism in history shown throughout the text.
